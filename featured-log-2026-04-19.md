### 2. Fitness Interview — Training technique adaptation for injury
- **Deadline:** 11d 17h (expires ~2026-05-01)
- **Publication:** Fitness Interview
- **Question:** "How have you successfully adapted a training technique to accommodate an injury while still making progress? What key modification made this possible without compromising recovery?"

Draft response (165 words):
> Our research team at WolveStack frequently reviews case studies where athletes maintain progress around injury, and one modification stands out: switching from bilateral to unilateral loading on the unaffected side. When a trainee hits a knee, shoulder, or hip issue, the intact limb can continue to train — and the contralateral effect means the injured side retains 15-25% of its strength through neural transfer alone, even with zero direct loading.
>
> The key implementation detail: match the rep ranges and tempo you'd use bilaterally, not the load. A trainee doing 3x8 back squats prior to injury switches to 3x8 goblet step-ups or Bulgarian split squats on the healthy leg. Range of motion and tempo are preserved; the absolute load drops, but the stimulus quality doesn't.
>
> Progress isn't compromised because the recovery-limiting factor (systemic fatigue plus local tissue stress on the injury) is actually reduced, allowing rehab to accelerate while the training signal stays intact.

### 3. PharmaTech News — Staying active when unmotivated
- **Deadline:** 10d 9h (expires ~2026-04-29)
- **Publication:** PharmaTech News
- **Question:** "How do you stay active and incorporate exercise into your routine, even on days when you lack motivation? What's your secret?"

Draft response (145 words):
> At WolveStack, our research team's rule is that motivation is a lagging indicator, not a leading one — waiting to feel motivated is the trap. The single highest-leverage tactic we recommend is the "two-minute floor": on low-motivation days, commit only to two minutes of movement. Walk outside. Do one warm-up set. Ninety percent of the time, momentum takes over and the full session happens; the other ten percent, you still got two minutes of movement in.
>
> The second rule is pairing exercise with an already-automatic trigger — morning coffee, end of the workday, after school drop-off. Habit stacking eliminates the decision point where motivation gets consulted.
>
> Finally, track something tiny. A single checkmark per session on a wall calendar. The visual streak becomes its own motivator, and the brain treats breaking the chain as more aversive than doing the workout.
