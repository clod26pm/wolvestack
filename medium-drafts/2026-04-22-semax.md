# I Went Looking for a Cleaner Focus Compound. I Ended Up Reading Russian Stroke Trials.

That's the short version of how I found Semax. The longer version involves a string of failed stimulants, a productive year on Selank, and the slow realization that anxiolytics alone don't make you sharp — they just make you less scattered.

If you're deep enough in the nootropic world to have tried Selank, you've probably brushed up against its stimulating cousin. But most of the English-language coverage of Semax reads like a PubMed citation dump or a research chemical reseller's product page. Almost nobody explains the thing that actually matters: Semax has been used in Russian hospitals for acute stroke since the 1990s. It's not a grey-market molecule pulled off a 2010s bodybuilding forum. It was developed at the Institute of Molecular Genetics at the Russian Academy of Sciences, derived from a 7-amino-acid fragment of ACTH with the adrenal activity stripped out.

That context reframes everything, because it tells you the mechanism isn't speculative.

**What Semax actually does**

The short mechanistic story is that Semax upregulates BDNF — brain-derived neurotrophic factor — in the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex. BDNF is the molecular signal that builds new synapses. When your BDNF is low, you feel foggy, slow, and stuck. When it's elevated, learning feels easier and memory consolidates better. This isn't a gym-bro oversimplification; neuroscience has been writing about BDNF for two decades. What makes Semax unusual is that rather than delivering exogenous BDNF (which can't cross the blood-brain barrier anyway), it stimulates the brain to make its own. That's a rare property in a synthetic peptide.

Secondarily, Semax modulates the monoamine systems — dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine. That's the part you feel on day one. The BDNF-driven stuff takes longer, closer to 2–3 weeks of consistent dosing before the "I can actually hold three ideas in my head" effect shows up.

**The stroke data is the part nobody talks about**

Here's where the Russian clinical history changes how I think about this peptide. In acute ischemic stroke, neurons die from excitotoxicity, oxidative stress, and inflammatory cascades. Russian groups — primarily Ashmarin's lab and follow-on researchers — showed Semax given intranasally within hours of stroke onset reduced infarct size and improved neurological recovery by roughly 15–30% versus controls. These weren't hand-wavy mouse experiments. They were human patients with MRIs and standardized neurological scales.

Most neuroprotectants that work in animal stroke models fail in humans. Semax is one of the few that translated. I'm not going to pretend I understand every variable in the Russian-language literature, but the consistency of those stroke results is what pushed me from "interesting molecule" to "I want to understand this properly."

**Running it: what actually happens**

I started on NA-Semax — the N-acetylated version. Standard Semax has a half-life of maybe 15–30 minutes, which is why people dose it 2–3 times daily. Acetylating the N-terminus protects it from exopeptidases and stretches the half-life to closer to 60–90 minutes. The amidated version (NA-Semax Amidate) goes longer still, but it's also the most expensive, and I wasn't ready to jump to the premium tier before I knew how I'd respond.

My protocol was 150 mcg intranasally, once in the morning. Week one, I noticed roughly 20% more staying-power on hard reading tasks. Not a stimulant high — more like the gap between my intention and my attention got smaller. Week two was the interesting one. Around day 10 I was retaining structured information — podcasts, technical articles — better than my baseline. That's the BDNF effect showing up on the timeline the mechanism would predict.

The thing people underplay: Semax is subtly stimulating. If you dose it past noon, you'll regret it at bedtime. It also thinned my appetite for a week or so — not dramatically, but noticeably. And the nasal route matters. Injected Semax works, but the intranasal form reaches CNS tissue via the olfactory pathway, which is partly why the Russian stroke protocols use drops rather than IM or IV.

**Where Selank comes back in**

The thing I wish someone had told me earlier: Semax and Selank are designed to be run together. Semax pushes cognition and focus; Selank rounds off the stimulatory edges and handles anxiety through GABA-A modulation. Stacking them is the default Russian community protocol, and in my experience it's correct. Semax alone at higher doses can leave you a bit wired. Selank dissolves that without blunting the focus. **Honest limitations**

Semax isn't FDA-approved. It's a research chemical in the US. The English literature is thin, and most of the strong clinical evidence is in Russian, which introduces translation and methodology questions that some researchers find legitimate. The BDNF-over-time concern — that chronic elevation might downregulate TrkB — is speculative but plausible enough that the community cycles 2–4 weeks on, followed by equal or greater rest periods.
