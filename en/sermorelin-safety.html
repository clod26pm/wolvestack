Sermorelin Safety

Side Effects, Risks & Safety Profile

📖 8 min read 🔬 Research & Education ⚠️ Not medical advice ✅ Last reviewed: April 2026

Medical Disclaimer

For informational and educational purposes only. Not FDA-approved for human use. Consult a licensed healthcare professional. See full disclaimer.

Sermorelin safety profile is favorable: most users experience no serious adverse effects. Common mild side effects: water retention (30-40%), joint pain/arthralgia (10-15%), carpal tunnel syndrome (2-5%), numbness/tingling (5-10%), transient cortisol elevation (self-resolving). Serious adverse effects are rare: no documented cancers, cardiovascular events, or liver toxicity in published research. Contraindications: active cancer (relative), uncontrolled diabetes, severe hypertension. Long-term safety data (24+ months) is limited; cycling is recommended.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is sermorelin safe overall?

Yes. Safety profile is favorable for short-term use. Most side effects are mild and dose-dependent. Long-term (24+ month) safety is incompletely characterized.

What are the most common side effects?

Water retention (30-40%), joint pain (10-15%), carpal tunnel (2-5%), numbness/tingling (5-10%). Most resolve within 1-2 weeks of discontinuation.

Does sermorelin increase cancer risk?

No evidence of increased cancer risk in published studies. GH itself has been questioned in some contexts, but sermorelin's gentle GH stimulation is lower-risk than exogenous HGH.

Can sermorelin cause diabetes?

Sermorelin may slightly impair glucose tolerance in some users (GH is diabetogenic at high doses). Minimal risk at research doses; monitor glucose if diabetic.

What should I do if I get carpal tunnel?

Reduce dose by 25-50%, take nightly breaks (2-3 days off per week), use wrist splinting, consider discontinuation if severe. Symptoms resolve within 1-2 weeks after stopping.

Who shouldn't use sermorelin?

Active cancer (relative contraindication), uncontrolled diabetes, severe untreated hypertension. Otherwise-healthy individuals tolerate sermorelin well.
