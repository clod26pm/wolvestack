# Peptide Content Batch — April 16, 2026

## Trend Report

### Trend 1: FDA Advisory Panel Set for July 23-24 to Decide BPC-157 and TB-500 Compounding Fate

Yesterday (April 15), multiple major outlets — Washington Post, ABC News, STAT News, NBC — broke the news that the FDA has officially scheduled its Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee meeting for July 23-24, 2026. The panel will specifically discuss whether BPC-157, TB-500, KPV, and MOTs-C should be added back to the 503A Bulks List, which would allow licensed compounding pharmacies to manufacture them with a valid prescription. This follows HHS Secretary RFK Jr.'s February announcement to reclassify 14 of 19 previously restricted peptides. This is the single biggest regulatory event for the peptide industry in 2026 — the outcome will directly impact availability, pricing, and legitimacy of the most popular healing peptides.

### Trend 2: Stanford's "Natural Ozempic" (BRP Peptide) Discovery Published in Nature

Stanford researchers published a groundbreaking study in Nature identifying BRP (BRINP2-Related Peptide), a naturally occurring molecule that mimics semaglutide's appetite suppression but works exclusively in the hypothalamus. In animal studies, a single BRP injection reduced food intake by up to 50% within an hour — without causing nausea, constipation, or muscle loss (the top three complaints about GLP-1 drugs). The discovery used AI to scan prohormones. A company has been founded to pursue human trials. This is massive for the weight-loss peptide conversation and provides a compelling alternative narrative to the current GLP-1 dominance.

### Trend 3: First Human Safety Data for IV BPC-157 Published

A pilot study (PubMed ID: 40131143) has been published showing results from intravenous BPC-157 infusions in human subjects. The study found no measurable effects on biomarkers of heart, liver, kidneys, thyroid, or blood glucose — and the infusion was tolerated with no reported side effects. While this is a small pilot, it represents one of the first pieces of formal human safety data for BPC-157, which has been a major gap in the evidence base that critics (and the FDA) consistently point to.
