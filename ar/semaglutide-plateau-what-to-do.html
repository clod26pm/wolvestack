Weight loss plateau typically occurs at 24-36 weeks when 15-22% body weight is lost. Causes: metabolic adaptation (decreased energy expenditure, increased hunger hormones), caloric intake creep, and homeostatic resistance. Breaking plateau: increase exercise intensity/frequency, reduce caloric intake further, or add complementary medication (phentermine, naltrexone/bupropion).

Why Weight Loss Plateaus

Biological adaptation: the body resists weight loss through multiple mechanisms. Metabolic rate decreases 10-25% with sustained caloric deficit and weight loss (metabolic adaptation). This is adaptive thermogenesis—the body preserves energy to resist starvation. Appetite hormones: ghrelin increases and leptin decreases with weight loss, driving hunger increase. Hunger becomes difficult to ignore without continued appetite suppression from semaglutide. Caloric intake creep: over weeks, portions gradually increase as nausea resolves and appetite suppression is less pronounced. Small increases (10-20 extra calories daily) accumulate to substantial weekly surplus. Increased physical activity: paradoxically, some individuals unconsciously increase activity or muscle use during weight loss (spontaneous physical activity), burning more calories. Weight loss asymptote: most individuals reach a 'set point' weight where caloric intake (at near-satiation) equals energy expenditure; further loss requires disrupting this equilibrium.

Timeline of Plateau Development

Week 1-24: active weight loss phase, 1-2 lbs/week. Week 24-36: weight loss slows but continues, 0.5-1 lb/week. Week 36-52: plateau begins; weight change <0.25 lbs/week. After 52 weeks: weight typically stable at new equilibrium. Duration of plateau varies: some individuals plateau for months without further loss; others resume gradual loss after temporary plateau. Most clinicians consider a 4-week period without weight change (>2 lbs) a true plateau warranting intervention.

Metabolic Adaptation Physiology

Basal metabolic rate (BMR) reduction: initial 10-15% decrease within weeks of weight loss initiation, further 10-15% decrease by 6 months. This is driven by: reduced tissue mass (less energy required to maintain smaller body), hormonal changes (reduced thyroid hormone, increased cortisol), and central nervous system adaptation (brain signals preservation mode). Adaptive thermogenesis: brown adipose tissue activation and uncoupling protein (UCP) efficiency changes reduce energy expenditure. Physical activity energy expenditure: same workout burns fewer calories at lower body weight (a 180-lb person burns ~25% fewer calories jogging than a 220-lb person does the same jog). Ghrelin dynamics: fasting ghrelin levels increase with weight loss; this drives persistent hunger despite semaglutide's appetite suppression. Leptin resistance: falling leptin concentrations and potential CNS leptin resistance reduces satiation signaling.

Strategies to Break Plateau

Exercise intensification: increase resistance training frequency to 4-5x weekly, increase aerobic exercise to 200-300 min/week, or increase intensity (higher resistance, higher heart rate). Exercise creates metabolic stress and increases EPOC (excess post-exercise oxygen consumption), burning additional calories. Caloric deficit increase: reduce intake by 200-300 calories daily (more aggressive deficit). With semaglutide's continued appetite suppression, this is often achievable without severe hunger. Dietary composition: increase protein to 1.2-1.4 g/kg (increases thermic effect of food), reduce refined carbs, increase fiber (improves satiety). Sleep optimization: inadequate sleep (<6 hours) increases hunger hormones and decreases satiety hormones; prioritizing 7-9 hours nightly supports weight loss. Stress reduction: high cortisol from stress impairs weight loss; meditation, yoga, or therapy can help.

Pharmacological Adjuncts

Phentermine: sympathomimetic appetite suppressant; 15-37.5 mg daily can be combined with semaglutide for synergistic appetite suppression. Cost: $20-50/month; limited to 12 weeks continuous use FDA-approved (though sometimes prescribed longer). Naltrexone/bupropion (Contrave): combination of opioid antagonist + antidepressant that enhances appetite suppression and increases energy expenditure. Can be combined with semaglutide though evidence is limited. Cost: $200-300/month. Topiramate: anticonvulsant with appetite-suppressing effects; 50-100 mg daily may enhance weight loss. Cost: $10-30/month generic. GLP-1 dose optimization: confirming current dose (2.4 mg) is adequate; some individuals benefit from dose frequency change (e.g., increasing to 2.4 mg every 5-6 days instead of weekly).
