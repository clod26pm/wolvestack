Frustrating when vendors can't even explain their own products. You're definitely not the first person I've seen deal with this.

Here's the key thing most people don't realize: there's no single "correct" amount of bacteriostatic water for a 12mg vial. The water you add just determines the concentration, which then determines how much liquid you draw up per dose. More water = lower concentration = more liquid per injection = easier to measure small doses accurately.

The math is simple once you see it. Say you add 2ml of bac water to your 12mg vial. That gives you 6mg/ml, or 6,000 mcg per ml. If your target dose is 300 mcg, you'd draw 0.05 ml — that's 5 units on a standard U-100 insulin syringe. Honestly? That's tiny and hard to measure precisely.

Add 4ml instead, and you get 3mg/ml (3,000 mcg/ml). That same 300 mcg dose becomes 0.1 ml — 10 units on the syringe. Much easier to read without squinting.

My rule of thumb: use enough water so your typical dose lands between 10-20 units on an insulin syringe. For a 12mg vial, 2-4ml of bac water covers most dosing protocols comfortably.

The actual reconstitution technique matters too. Draw the bac water into the syringe, then inject it slowly along the inside wall of the vial — don't blast it directly onto the peptide cake. Then gently swirl. Never shake. Most peptides dissolve within a couple minutes. If the solution is still cloudy after 10 minutes of gentle swirling, something might be off with the product.

After reconstitution, store in the fridge (not freezer). Reconstituted peptides with bacteriostatic water generally stay stable for 3-4 weeks refrigerated.

**Question:** "Why is the FDA banning the substance BPC-157?"
**URL:** https://www.quora.com/Why-is-the-FDA-banning-the-substance-BPC-157
**Status:** Has some answers — room for a factual, nuanced response

**Answer:**

The FDA didn't technically "ban" BPC-157 — the distinction matters. What happened is the FDA added BPC-157 to its list of substances that cannot be used in compounded medications under federal law. Compounding pharmacies, which were previously the main legitimate source for people getting BPC-157 through clinics and prescribers, can no longer produce it.

The reasoning: BPC-157 has never been through FDA-approved clinical trials in humans. Under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, compounding pharmacies are only supposed to work with substances that are components of FDA-approved drugs or appear on a specific bulk ingredient list. BPC-157 qualifies as neither.

This came as part of a broader crackdown on the peptide compounding industry that accelerated through late 2024. The FDA nominated several peptides for review, and BPC-157 didn't make the cut. Their stated concern was that without completed human trials, the safety and efficacy profile isn't established to a standard they're comfortable with for prescribing.

Now, there's a legitimate frustration here. The animal research on BPC-157 is extensive — hundreds of published studies showing tissue repair, anti-inflammatory effects, neuroprotection, and gut healing across multiple injury models. But the FDA's position is that animal data alone isn't sufficient for human use through medical channels. They're not wrong about the regulatory principle, even if the practical effect is pushing people toward less regulated research chemical markets.

The irony is that the crackdown probably made things less safe, not more. When people could get pharmaceutical-grade BPC-157 through licensed compounding pharmacies with physician oversight, there was at least a quality control layer. Now the sourcing is almost entirely through research peptide vendors of varying reliability.

BPC-157 is still legal to purchase for research purposes. It's not a controlled substance. The restriction specifically targets compounding pharmacies and clinical prescribing.
